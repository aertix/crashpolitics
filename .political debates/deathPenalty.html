Death Penalty Debate

The death penalty is the execution of felons who have committed crimes so cruel that a jury unanimously (everyone agrees) decides to sentence the accused to death.

Historically the death penalty was carried out through electric chairs, hanging, or firing squad, which led to a painful death. However, in modern criminal justice, the death penalty is performed through lethal injection, which is a combination of chemicals injected into the criminal to stop the heart.

The death penalty was instituted as a harsher punishment for the worst criminals. The death penalty is legal in 27 states, and the 23 remaining do not sentence criminals to death.

The death penalty has been a major ethical dilemma across American politics.

Supporters of the death penalty claim the following:

Those who take a life have given up their right to live, justifying death as a criminal sentence. This can also be seen as the “an eye for an eye” ideology.

The more extreme punishment of death is more effective in setting a precedent and preventing more people from committing horrific crimes. This belief claims that life in prison does not deter people who have committed crimes enough from committing more violent crimes.

The death penalty gives more closure to the families of the victim(s).

Those who want to abolish the death penalty argue the following:

It is illogical to punish a person for murder by killing them. This reasoning is contradictory.

Carrying out an execution is more than double the price of keeping an offender in prison for life. This costs more for the taxpayers, which can easily be reduced through the abolition of capital punishment.

Realistically, if life in prison cannot deter an offender from crime, then the death penalty will not be able to deter more crime. Therefore, the argument that the death penalty prevents more crime is unjustifiable.

Statistics show that minorities are disproportionally put on death row and executed due to systemic racism, proving that the death penalty system is flawed.
